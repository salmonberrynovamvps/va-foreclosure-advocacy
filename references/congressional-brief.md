# Congressional Brief Style

## When to use
Drafting one-pagers, advocacy briefs, emails to congressional offices,
testimony, public comments, or any document for a legislative audience.

## What it optimizes for
Formal but accessible policy communication. Numbers over adjectives.
Every claim backed by a specific date, regulation, or data point.
Specific asks at the end.

## Style rules

Structure:
- Lead with the issue in 2 sentences maximum
- Use clear section headers: ISSUE, KEY FACTS, VIOLATIONS, THE ASK
- Bullet points for facts — one fact per bullet, with date or citation
- Tables for comparisons (cost analysis, timeline, violation summary)
- Bold for names, dates, dollar amounts, and regulation numbers
- Keep to 1 page when possible

Language:
- Numbers over adjectives
- Specific over general
- Active voice
- No emotional language in the lead
- Plain language for legal terms with parenthetical definitions

Tone:
- Professional and factual
- Urgent but measured
- Respectful of the office's time
- Confidence without aggression

Closing:
- Every document ends with a specific, actionable ASK
- Offer to provide additional documentation on request
- Include contact information
