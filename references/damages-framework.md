### Pattern or practice enhancement

If the servicer's conduct is part of a **pattern or practice** (not
isolated), statutory damages increase. The servicer's enforcement history
(see Step 9) provides pattern evidence.

**Class action potential:** In class actions, statutory damages can be
up to $1,000,000 or 1% of the servicer's net worth. `[VERIFY current
amounts]`

### Evidence needed

- Documentation of each specific RESPA violation
- Dates of violation
- Servicer's enforcement history showing pattern

## 4. Consequential Damages

These are damages that flow from the foreclosure as a consequence of the
servicer's violations. Each must be documented with evidence showing the
causal connection.

### Categories

| Category | What to document | Typical evidence |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Relocation costs** | Moving expenses, temporary housing, storage, deposits | Receipts, lease agreements, moving company invoices |
| **Health impacts** | Exacerbation of service-connected conditions, mental health impacts of housing instability | VA medical records (with consent), provider statements |
| **Credit damage** | Higher interest rates on future borrowing, denied applications, increased insurance costs due to credit score | Credit reports, denial letters, rate quotes showing credit-score impact |
| **Lost benefits** | Lost property tax exemption, lost VA housing benefits, lost homestead protections | County records, VA benefit statements |
| **Employment impact** | Lost work time managing the case, inability to work due to housing instability | Pay stubs, employer statements, time records |
| **Child/family impact** | School disruption, counseling costs, loss of stability | School records, counseling receipts, relocation records |
| **Emergency costs** | Costs incurred due to sudden displacement threat | Receipts for emergency planning, storage, travel |

### Causal chain requirement

For each consequential damage, document:

1. **The violation** — which servicer action/inaction caused this
2. **The connection** — how the violation led to this specific harm
3. **The damage** — dollar amount or quantifiable impact
4. **The evidence** — receipts, records, statements proving the harm

## 6. Fee-Shifting / Attorney Fees

### Statutes providing fee recovery

| Statute | Provision | Requirement |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| RESPA §2605(f) | Attorney fees for prevailing borrower | Must establish a RESPA violation |
| State CPA (varies) | Attorney fees for prevailing plaintiff | Must establish CPA violation |
| FCRA | Attorney fees for willful violations | Must establish willful FCRA violation |

### Why fee-shifting matters for advocacy

Fee-shifting provisions make it economically viable for attorneys to take
the case on contingency or limited-fee basis. When pitching the case to
potential counsel, **lead with fee-shifting availability** — this is what
makes the case financially workable for a pro bono or contingency attorney.

## 7. VSO Payment Interference

### Calculation

```
A. Correct reinstatement amount (without errors):   $________
B. Actual reinstatement amount quoted by servicer:   $________
C. Inflation amount (B - A):                         $________
D. VSO/third-party payment made:                     $________
E. Shortfall (B - D):                                $________
F. Would payment have cured if amount was correct?   YES / NO
```

### If YES — the error defeated the cure

This is the most powerful damages argument for advocacy purposes. The
chain is:

1. Servicer error inflated the reinstatement figure by $C
2. Third party paid $D toward the inflated figure of $B
3. Payment fell short by $E
4. If the correct amount had been $A, the payment of $D would have cured
5. The servicer's error — not the borrower's inability to pay — caused
   the foreclosure

### Evidence needed

- VSO/third-party correspondence confirming payment amount and date
- Servicer-provided reinstatement figure that the payment targeted
- Escrow overcharge calculation showing the inflation
- Confirmation that correct reinstatement would have been within the
  payment amount

## 8. Fiscal Impact to Government

This section is for **congressional advocacy framing**, not litigation
damages. It shows the cost of foreclosure to the taxpayer.

### Per-family foreclosure cost to government

| Cost category | Estimated amount | Source |
|--------------|-----------------|--------|
| VA Loan Guaranty Fund (direct disposition) | ~$72,000 per foreclosure | Cohen testimony, March 26 HVAC hearing |
| VA healthcare (increased utilization post-displacement) | ~$15,000-30,000/year | VA healthcare cost data `[VERIFY]` |
| HUD-VASH / homeless services | ~$20,000-40,000/year | HUD per-unit cost data `[VERIFY]` |
| SSVF / emergency assistance | ~$5,000-10,000 per episode | SSVF program data `[VERIFY]` |
| ER visits (5 visits × avg cost) | ~$18,500/year | Hospital cost data `[VERIFY]` |
| State/local services | Variable | Varies by jurisdiction |

### Comparison: foreclosure vs. partial claim

| Option | Cost to VA | Cost to borrower |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Partial claim** | $0 immediate outlay (repaid at sale/refi) | Stays in home, subordinate lien |
| **Foreclosure → REO** | ~$72,000 direct + downstream costs | Loses home, enters housing crisis |

### The fiscal irrationality argument

**For Republican/fiscal-conservative members:** VA foreclosure costs the
Loan Guaranty Fund ~$72K per disposition. 75% of VA foreclosures go to
REO. Partial claim costs VA nothing in immediate outlays. VA is cost-
shifting across VA healthcare, HUD-VASH, homeless prevention, and other
federal/state programs. Total 3-year government cost per foreclosed
veteran family: approximately $195,000+.
