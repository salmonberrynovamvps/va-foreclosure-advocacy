# Political Strategy Guide

Reference file for `va-foreclosure-case-analyzer` SKILL.md, Step 10.

How to assess the political landscape and calibrate advocacy framing
to the target audience's political psychology.

**Core principle:** The facts never change. The frame does. Every
document should be factually identical regardless of audience — only
the emphasis and lead change.

## 1. Political Landscape Assessment

Before choosing targets and framing, assess these factors:

| Factor | What to check | Where to find it |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Session status** | Is Congress in session or recess? | congress.gov/schedules |
| **Committee hearings** | Any upcoming hearings on VA, housing, mortgage servicing? | committee websites, govtrack.us |
| **Appropriations cycle** | Is VA/HUD funding being debated? | Appropriations committee calendars |
| **Pending legislation** | Any bills in play on foreclosure, VA loans, partial claim? | congress.gov, govtrack.us |
| **Pending rulemaking** | VA drafting table, CFPB proposed rules, M26-4 revisions? | federalregister.gov, va.gov |
| **Agency leadership** | Who runs CFPB? VA Loan Guaranty? State AG consumer protection? | Agency websites |
| **Media cycle** | Active coverage of VA housing issues? Reporter working on a story? | News search, reporter contacts |

## 2. Partisan Framing Matrix

Same case. Different emphasis. Match the audience.

### Republican / Conservative members

**Lead with:**
- Failed promise to a Marine / veteran betrayal
- Government waste — VA now holds a house it shouldn't have had to acquire
- VA system didn't protect its own
- Fiscal irrationality — foreclosure costs taxpayers more than alternatives

**Supporting data:**
- $72K per foreclosure disposition (Cohen testimony)
- 75% of VA foreclosures go to REO (Auction.com data)
- Partial claim costs $0 immediate vs. $195K+ total government cost per
  foreclosed family over 3 years

**Language choices:**
- "Promise kept" / "promise broken"
- "Taxpayer dollars wasted"
- "The system had the tools and didn't use them"
- "This Marine served his country. The VA failed to serve him."

**What NOT to lead with:**
- Systemic racism framing as the headline (can include as supporting data)
- "Corporate greed" as the primary villain (frame as system failure instead)
- Partisan blame for the policy gap

---

### Democratic / Progressive members

**Lead with:**
- Disparate impact on disabled veteran family
- Corporate pattern of abuse by a repeat-offender servicer
- Housing equity failure
- Regulatory gap that Congress was supposed to fix

**Supporting data:**
- Servicer's enforcement history ($130M+ in penalties)
- HMDA data on disparate outcomes if available
- CFPB repeat offender designation
- Equity impact statement from Step 4

**Language choices:**
- "Held accountable"
- "Pattern of abuse"
- "Protecting the most vulnerable"
- "The regulations exist — they weren't enforced"

**What NOT to lead with:**
- "Government is the problem" framing
- Anti-regulation arguments
- Fiscal-only framing without equity context

---

### Bipartisan / Moderate members

**Lead with:**
- Oversight failure — the system had tools and didn't use them
- Taxpayer cost (fiscal) + family impact (human)
- VALERI was built to catch this and it didn't work
- Concrete fix: make the system do what it was designed to do

**Supporting data:**
- Both fiscal cost data AND equity/human impact
- VA OIG's own prior audit findings confirming oversight gaps
- The specific, fixable structural problem (VALERI non-submission gap)

**Language choices:**
- "Accountability"
- "The system failed"
- "Fixable problem"
- "This shouldn't happen to any veteran"

## 3. Committee Jurisdiction Map

### Primary committees

| Committee | Jurisdiction | What they can do |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **House Veterans Affairs (HVAC)** | VA programs, veteran benefits, VA oversight | Hearings, investigations, legislation, constituent intervention |
| — Economic Opportunity subcommittee | VA housing, loan guaranty, homelessness | Most directly relevant subcommittee |
| — Oversight & Investigations subcommittee | VA accountability, waste/fraud | Can investigate VA oversight failures |
| **Senate Veterans Affairs (SVAC)** | Same as HVAC, Senate side | Same powers, Senate side |
| **House Financial Services** | Mortgage regulation, CFPB oversight, housing policy | Can pressure CFPB on enforcement |
| — Housing subcommittee | Mortgage servicing, foreclosure policy | Direct jurisdiction over servicer conduct |
| **Senate Banking** | Same as House Financial Services, Senate side | CFPB oversight, housing policy |

### Secondary committees

| Committee | When relevant |
|-----------|--------------|
| **House/Senate Appropriations — MilCon/VA** | When VA funding is being debated (leverage for oversight riders) |
| **House/Senate Judiciary** | If fair lending / civil rights claims are involved |

### Key contacts

- **Committee staff** handle policy and oversight — they're the people
  who decide what the committee investigates
- **Member's personal office** handles constituent services — they
  submit individual case inquiries
- **Both matter.** Committee staff can drive systemic action. Personal
  office staff can drive individual case resolution.

## 4. Agency Enforcement Posture

Agency leadership changes how effectively complaints land.

### Assessment framework

| Agency | Questions to assess | Implications |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **CFPB** | Is the current director enforcement-aggressive or hands-off? Has the agency been filing new enforcement actions? Are repeat offender designations being maintained? | Aggressive → file with CFPB and expect action. Passive → congressional pressure to force CFPB action may be more effective. |
| **VA Loan Guaranty** | Is VA leadership prioritizing foreclosure prevention? Is partial claim implementation being expedited? | Active leadership → direct engagement may work. Passive → congressional pressure needed. |
| **State AG** | Is the AG's consumer protection division actively pursuing mortgage cases? Has this AG filed against mortgage servicers before? | Active → file and expect engagement. Inactive → your complaint could be the catalyst for a new enforcement area. Frame as opportunity. |
| **VA OIG** | Is OIG investigating loan guaranty issues? Have they published recent reports on servicer oversight? | Active → your complaint supplements their existing work. Reference their prior findings. |

### Critical note

Agency enforcement posture can change rapidly — new appointments,
new priorities, budget changes. Build strategy that works regardless
of who's in charge. The underlying regulatory violations exist
independent of enforcement climate.

## 5. Election Cycle Considerations

| Cycle position | How it affects advocacy |
|---------------|----------------------|
| **Pre-primary** (6+ months before) | Members are establishing records. "Protecting veterans" is universally popular. Responsive to constituent stories that become campaign material. |
| **Primary season** | Members in competitive primaries are highly responsive to constituent visibility. A veteran family losing their home is politically dangerous to ignore. |
| **General election** | Same dynamics as primary but broader audience. Both parties want the veteran vote. |
| **Lame duck** (post-election, pre-inauguration) | Outgoing members may take bold action without electoral consequence. Incoming members may want early wins. |
| **Off-cycle** | Least responsive period. Policy work happens here — committee hearings, rulemaking comments, long-term investigations. |

### Veteran-specific dynamics

- **Both parties compete for the veteran vote** — this is one of the
  few genuinely bipartisan constituencies
- **"Veteran family loses home"** is a story no legislator wants
  associated with their inaction
- **VSO endorsements matter** — if WWP, DAV, VFW, or American Legion
  are engaged, the political weight increases significantly

## 6. Timing and Sequencing

### Sequencing categories

| Category | Examples | Rule |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Send NOW** | SOL-urgent claims, pending deadlines (Cash for Keys, eviction), imminent hearings | Legal deadlines always override political timing |
| **Coordinate** | Congressional inquiry + CFPB complaint + media story | More powerful together than separately. Aim for same week if possible. |
| **Hold for timing** | Novel legal argument (§3720(h)), enforcement history reveal, systemic pattern analysis | Strategic assets — deploy when they'll have maximum impact (during a hearing, in response to a regulatory proposal, when media attention is high) |
| **Depends on external events** | Pending hearing schedule, NPR publication timing, rulemaking comment period, election calendar | Monitor and be ready to move fast when the window opens |

### Coordination principles

- **Congressional inquiry BEFORE or AFTER complaint?** Both work.
  Before: gives the office a heads-up. After: gives them something
  concrete to reference. Best: same week.

- **Media BEFORE or AFTER filing?** Usually after — the filing is a
  documented record the reporter can reference. Exception: if the
  reporter's story can pressure faster agency response, coordinate
  timing.

- **Public comment + case filing:** If there's an open rulemaking
  comment period (M26-4 Ch.5, CFPB proposed rules), your case
  becomes a real-world example. Time the comment to the period.
  Time the complaint filing to amplify the comment.

### The one unbreakable rule

**Legal deadlines always override political timing.** Never hold a
SOL-urgent filing for better optics. A technically perfect complaint
filed one day late is worth nothing.
