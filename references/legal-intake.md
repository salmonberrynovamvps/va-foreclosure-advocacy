# Legal Intake Style

## When to use
Attorney-facing documents: legal intake summaries, case analysis for
potential counsel, litigation memos, demand letter drafts, or any
document where the reader is a practicing attorney.

## What it optimizes for
Elements-focused analysis. Procedural posture. Citation-heavy.
Counterargument structure. Assumes legal literacy. The reader is
evaluating whether to take the case — give them what they need to decide.

## Style rules

Structure:
- Lead with case posture: jurisdiction, parties, procedural status, key dates
- Organize by claim, not by chronology — attorneys think in claims
- For each claim: elements, evidence, anticipated defenses, rebuttal, damages, SOL status
- Use headers for each claim type
- Tables for evidence mapping and damages summary

Language:
- Legal terminology expected
- Full regulatory citations on first reference
- Case citations in standard format
- Precise language

Tone:
- Professional, analytical, objective
- Present the strongest version of each claim without overselling
- Acknowledge weaknesses — attorneys respect candor
- Rank claims clearly: "The strongest claim is X because..."

What attorneys need to see:
- Fee-shifting availability (can they get paid?)
- Damages range (is it worth their time?)
- Novel legal theories (is this interesting?)
- Existing complaints filed (what's the record?)
- Pattern evidence (is there a class action possibility?)
- Counterarguments they'll face and how to defeat them
