# Equity Assessment Guide

Reference file for `va-foreclosure-case-analyzer` SKILL.md, Step 4.

How to assess whether servicer violations produced disproportionate harm
based on protected characteristics. This is what transforms a technical
complaint into an enforcement priority.

**Consent rule:** Never pressure disclosure. Explain why this information
strengthens advocacy. Respect "no." Mark undisclosed factors as
"not assessed."

## Why Equity Assessment Matters

Agencies receive thousands of complaints. The ones that become
investigations share a common feature: **harm that falls on populations
the laws were specifically designed to protect.**

- CFPB prioritizes cases showing disparate impact by race, disability,
  or age
- VA OIG prioritizes cases involving disabled veterans and caregiver
  families
- Congressional offices respond strongest to constituent stories that
  illustrate systemic inequity
- Media covers stories where the human dimension intersects with policy
  failure

A complaint that says "servicer violated RESPA" goes in the general pile.

A complaint that says "servicer's RESPA violations caused a 100% T&P
disabled veteran's family — including minor children — to lose their
home in a rural area with no alternative housing, despite three
nonprofit organizations attempting to intervene" goes in the
enforcement priority pile.

Same facts. Different frame. The equity assessment builds that frame.

## Household Equity Profile

Gather with care. Use this as a conversation guide, not an interrogation.

### Protected class factors

| Factor | Questions to ask | Why it matters for advocacy |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Disability — veteran** | VA disability rating? Service-connected conditions? TBI/PTSD? Does the condition affect ability to self-advocate, process complex documents, or manage multi-step bureaucratic processes? | VA's mandate is to protect disabled veterans. Failure to protect a 100% T&P veteran is the strongest possible frame for VA OIG and congressional advocacy. |
| **Disability — spouse/household** | Does the spouse or any household member have disabilities? Is the spouse also the veteran's caregiver? | Caregiver burden means the case manager is simultaneously providing medical support — capacity constraints are structural, not a choice. Elizabeth Dole Foundation framing. |
| **Race / ethnicity** | Self-identified race/ethnicity of borrower and household. | Check servicer's HMDA data for disparate foreclosure rates by race in the same geography. CFPB and DOJ Civil Rights Division prioritize racial disparities. |
| **Gender** | Gender of the primary case manager/advocate. Is the household headed by a woman managing both caregiving and the foreclosure fight? | Women disproportionately bear the "second shift" of crisis management. Gendered labor framing resonates with progressive congressional offices. |
| **LGBTQ+** | If relevant and the household wishes to disclose. | Housing discrimination compounds foreclosure harm. Reduced access to family support networks in some communities. |
| **Age — elderly** | Are there elderly members (65+) in the household? | Displacement mortality risk is higher for elderly individuals. Multigenerational home disruption affects people who cannot independently relocate. |
| **Age — children** | Minor children in the household? Ages? | School disruption, ACEs (adverse childhood experiences), loss of stability. Children cannot advocate for themselves. Strongest emotional leverage in congressional and media contexts. |
| **National origin / language** | Primary language? English proficiency? Immigration status considerations? | Language barriers compound bureaucratic complexity. Check whether servicer provided communications in the borrower's primary language. |

### Structural factors

| Factor | Questions to ask | Why it matters |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Multigenerational household** | Extended family living in the home? How many people displaced by foreclosure? | Foreclosure displaces not just the borrower but family members with no independent housing options. Scale of harm is larger than it appears on paper. |
| **Rural vs. urban** | Location type? Distance to nearest VA facility? Availability of alternative housing? Number of rental listings in the area? | Rural veterans have fewer options. Foreclosure in a tight rural market may mean leaving the community, losing proximity to VA healthcare, and severing social ties. |
| **Support network** | Extended family nearby? Faith community? VSO chapter access? Friends who can help? Or isolated? | Isolation means no safety net. The foreclosure system assumes the borrower has support — when they don't, every failure cascades further. |
| **Economic context** | First-generation homeowner? No family wealth safety net? Benefits-dependent income? Single income? | Distinguishes between "temporary hardship" and "permanent displacement." A family with savings and parents who can help recovers from foreclosure differently than a family with none of that. |
| **Caregiving load** | Is the primary advocate also the primary caregiver for the veteran and/or children? How many hours per day in caregiving? | Executive function budget is not unlimited. The person fighting the foreclosure may also be managing medications, medical appointments, school logistics, and household operations. This is context for why deadlines get missed and calls don't get returned. |

## Disparate Impact Analysis

### Step-by-step

**1. Identify which protected factors are present** (from profile above)

**2. For each factor, answer:**
- Did the servicer's specific conduct produce worse outcomes because of
  this factor?
- Did the regulatory system's design fail to account for this factor?
- Is there data showing this factor correlates with worse outcomes in
  mortgage servicing generally?

**3. Connect to specific violations:**

| Violation | How [factor] compounds the harm |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Loss mitigation failure | [e.g., "TBI impairs ability to navigate complex waterfall process; servicer did not provide accessible communication"] |
| Escrow overcharge | [e.g., "Disabled veteran property tax exemption was specifically designed to protect this population; failure to apply it is a failure of the protection itself"] |
| [etc.] | |

**4. Check for systemic pattern:**
- Does the servicer's HMDA data show disparate rates by race/ethnicity?
- Do CFPB complaints from other borrowers describe similar demographics?
- Has the servicer been cited for fair lending violations before?

## System Failure Equity Analysis

Beyond the servicer's conduct, did the regulatory system fail this
household inequitably?

### Questions to assess

- **VALERI design gap:** Did VALERI's reliance on servicer self-reporting
  fail veterans who can't self-advocate through alternative channels?
  A veteran with TBI who can't navigate phone trees and written
  correspondence is more dependent on the system working correctly.

- **Loss mitigation complexity:** Did the waterfall process assume
  financial sophistication the borrower didn't have? Did the servicer's
  communications use language accessible to the borrower?

- **Program transition timing:** Did the gap between VASP sunset and
  H.R. 1815 implementation fall hardest on the most vulnerable
  borrowers — those already in forbearance who couldn't absorb the
  transition?

- **Geographic barriers:** Did the borrower's rural location reduce
  access to legal aid, housing counselors, or VA facility staff who
  could have intervened?

- **Digital literacy:** Did the servicer's online-only processes
  exclude borrowers without reliable internet access or digital skills?

- **Language access:** Were communications available in the borrower's
  primary language?

## Data Sources for Pattern Evidence

Use these to support the equity assessment with systemic data.

| Source | What it shows | URL |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| **HMDA data** (CFPB) | Lending and servicing patterns by race, ethnicity, income, geography | ffiec.cfpb.gov/data-browser |
| **CFPB complaint database** | Complaints by company, product, demographics (when reported) | consumerfinance.gov/data-research/consumer-complaints |
| **Census / ACS data** | Demographic context for the borrower's area | data.census.gov |
| **VA disability data** | Disability rating distribution, service-connected conditions by demographic | va.gov/data |
| **HUD fair lending data** | Fair housing complaint patterns | hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp |
| **State AG reports** | Enforcement patterns, demographic data in settlements | [varies by state] |
| **Academic research** | Studies on racial disparities in mortgage servicing, foreclosure rates by disability status | Google Scholar, SSRN |

## Equity Impact Statement Templates

### Template A: For agency complaints (CFPB, OIG, state AG)

> The violations documented in this complaint produced disproportionate
> harm to a [protected characteristics] household. [Borrower/veteran]
> is a [disability rating] disabled veteran whose [specific condition]
> impairs [specific capacity relevant to navigating the servicing
> process]. The household includes [composition — children, elderly,
> caregiving structure].
>
> The servicer's failure to [specific violation] had compounding effects
> on this household because [connection between protected characteristic
> and amplified harm]. [Data point if available — e.g., "HMDA data shows
> the servicer forecloses on [demographic] borrowers at X times the rate
> of other borrowers in this geography."]
>
> This case illustrates a systemic pattern in which [broader equity
> frame]. We request that this complaint be evaluated not as an isolated
> servicing error but as evidence of conduct that disproportionately
> harms protected populations.

### Template B: For congressional advocacy

> [Veteran name]'s family represents exactly the population that
> [H.R. 1815 / the VA system / this committee's oversight] was designed
> to protect: a [disability rating] disabled [branch] veteran, [family
> composition], in [geographic context]. Despite [number] attempts by
> [VSOs/nonprofits] to prevent the foreclosure, the servicer's
> violations made rescue impossible.
>
> This is not a story about a family who couldn't pay their mortgage.
> It is a story about a system that failed a family it was built to
> protect — and failed them worse because of [specific equity factors].

### Template C: For media

> [First name] is a [brief human description — not a list of
> demographics, but a person]. [One sentence about the household].
> When [servicer] [violation in plain language], [consequence in human
> terms — not legal terms].
>
> [Organization/VSO] tried to help. [What happened — the rescue that
> failed]. The [specific servicer error] made the difference between
> keeping their home and losing it.

## Audience-Specific Equity Framing

| Audience | Lead equity frame | Supporting data | What NOT to lead with |
|----------|------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **CFPB** | Disparate impact data (HMDA, complaint demographics) | Pattern evidence across borrowers | Emotional narrative without data |
| **VA OIG** | Disabled veteran protection mandate; VALERI design gap | VA's own audit findings on oversight deficiencies | Fair lending framing (not OIG's jurisdiction) |
| **Congressional — R** | Failed promise to a Marine family; government waste from preventable foreclosure | Fiscal cost data ($72K+ per foreclosure) | Leading with racial disparity framing |
| **Congressional — D** | Disparate impact on disabled veteran family; corporate pattern; housing equity | HMDA data, enforcement history | "Government is the problem" framing |
| **Congressional — bipartisan** | System failure despite existing tools; oversight gap; taxpayer cost | Both fiscal and equity data | Strongly partisan framing either direction |
| **State AG** | Harm to state residents; state CPA violations; "your state could be first" | State-specific complaint data | Federal regulatory arguments (not their jurisdiction) |
| **Media** | Human story first, equity context second, policy failure third | One or two powerful specific details | Data-heavy lead; legal jargon |
| **Legal aid attorney** | Clean factual record; viable claims; fee-shifting; novel legal theory | Strength of evidence; damages potential | Emotional narrative without legal substance |

**Key principle:** The equity frame changes by audience. The facts never
change. Every document should be factually identical — only the emphasis
and framing differ.
