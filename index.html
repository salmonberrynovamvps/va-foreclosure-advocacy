Section 1

What's Happening — The Short Version

This didn't start with VASP. During COVID, the government told veterans to use forbearance — pause their mortgage payments. The VA had a partial claim program (COVID-VAPCP) that would let veterans defer missed payments as a zero-interest lien and resume paying at their original rate. Families were in active forbearance when this happened — no communication, no updates. Many found out the way we did: we called in December 2022 to restart our payments by February 1st, 2023, and were told the program was gone.

The options we were given: pay everything back immediately in a lump sum, or accept a loan modification that would change our contract — lose our 2.25% interest rate and see our payments increase over $1,000/month due to post-pandemic rate spikes. We were not allowed to just resume our payments. We were told this would be fixed. We waited.

And we couldn't even get help from the same place everyone else did. The Homeowner Assistance Fund (HAF) — the federal program created to help families who fell behind during COVID — disqualified us because of income eligibility limits. They counted VA disability compensation as income, even though we needed help because of the past, not the present. We had done exactly what we were told: pause payments temporarily, get back on our feet. I was working full-time. But the disability income that exists because my husband was injured serving pushed us over the limit. Every other pandemic relief program — PPP loans for businesses, HAF for homeowners with FHA and conventional loans — had better transitions and more options. Veterans got nothing.

Here's what makes this worse: every other federal mortgage program kept their safety net. FHA has a permanent partial claim. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac have payment deferrals. Only the VA let its program expire and left veterans with nothing. If you have an FHA loan and fall behind, the government helps you catch up at your original rate. If you have a VA loan — supposedly the benefit you earned by serving — you get a loan modification at current market rates, often 3x–4x your original. That's the gap.

VASP was built to fix that gap. It bought the loan from the servicer and modified it to 2.5% fixed. We were told this would fix everything. We got our paperwork in, we called and emailed and checked in, we waited through lengthy processing delays. VASP wasn't even fully operational until October 2024 — servicers had implementation delays for months. Then in May 2025, the VA killed VASP without a replacement ready. That was the second time veteran families had the rug pulled out from under them without notice.

We were told this would be fixed. Again. Second time now. Congress stepped in because two administrations had now done this to veterans. They passed H.R. 1815 (the VA Home Loan Program Reform Act) with unanimous bipartisan support — every Republican and every Democrat — signed into law July 30, 2025. It gives the VA permanent partial claim authority that can't be cancelled at the whim of any administration. But the VA still hasn't implemented it. New ETA: June 2026 — a full year after the law was signed.

This time, instead of cancelling a program, the VA has simply stalled — not implementing the law Congress passed, not complying with its own authority to prevent foreclosures, and not pausing evictions it has the legal power to stop. We waited this time without a moratorium or foreclosure pause. Veterans are losing their homes with no path to keep paying. The VA has the legal authority to pause all foreclosures and evictions right now under existing statute. They won't use it.

Section 2

How We Got Here — Both Administrations, Both Parties

The bipartisan bottom line

Biden's VA cancelled the first partial claim program (COVID-VAPCP) without notice while families were in active forbearance, then created VASP late. Trump's VA killed VASP without a replacement — the second time veteran families had the rug pulled — then failed to implement the law Congress passed to fix it. Congress did its job — unanimous vote, permanent law that can't be cancelled by any administration. The VA is the bottleneck. Instead of cancelling a program this time, they're simply not implementing the law and not stopping foreclosures they have the authority to stop. Government shutdowns, VA workforce cuts, and holiday recesses made it worse. This isn't left vs. right. This is 160,000+ families caught in a systemic failure that both administrations contributed to, that Congress fixed, and that the VA still won't act on.

This matches a broader GAO-documented pattern of VA non-implementation. Three GAO reports in early 2026 document parallel failures across veteran programs: GAO-26-107517 (3/30/26) found 174,000 homeless veterans were not referred to HUD-VASH supportive housing during 2020-2024, with VA failing to document the reason for 87% of cases. GAO-26-108943 (3/4/26) and GAO-26-108070 (4/16/26) document parallel implementation gaps in VA caregiver support tied to the Elizabeth Dole Caregiver Act. The VA mortgage foreclosure crisis is one front in a documented pattern of VA failing to implement bipartisan veteran-protection mandates.

Section 3

The Attorney Gap — Why Legal Action Matters

Right now, the biggest concrete gap is legal representation. Veteran families facing wrongful foreclosure need attorneys who specialize in RESPA/mortgage servicing litigation to:

  • File for injunctive relief — a court order forcing the VA to pause foreclosures/evictions until H.R. 1815 is implemented, or until the VA complies with its own internal circulars and statutory authority
  • Bring class action — represent the 10,000+ families who already lost homes and the 90,000+ currently in process, not just individual cases
  • Enforce existing law — H.R. 1815 is signed law. 38 U.S.C. § 3732 and § 3733 give the VA authority over properties it owns. If the VA won't act voluntarily, a judge can compel it

The Ledford family's local legal aid (Northwest Justice Project) was unable to take the case due to scope and capacity. This is the pattern — legal aid is overwhelmed, and private mortgage litigation attorneys are hard to find, especially in rural areas. If you are an attorney or know one who handles RESPA/VA mortgage cases, please reach out.

Section 4

Responding to Common Pushback

You'll see these arguments on social media. Here's the fact-based response for each.

"VASP was just a temporary COVID handout. It was always going to end."
VASP wasn't the start — it was the second attempt. The VA first had a COVID partial claim program (COVID-VAPCP), cancelled that without notice in 2022, then built VASP as the replacement, then cancelled that without notice in 2025. That's twice the VA pulled the safety net out from under veteran families. Meanwhile, FHA, Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac all kept their partial claim programs. The VA is the only federal mortgage system that doesn't have one. Congress passed the permanent fix (H.R. 1815) specifically because two administrations had cancelled programs at will. The VA still hasn't implemented it. You don't pull the net twice and call it "temporary."
"Veterans need to pay their mortgages and stop looking for handouts."
These families ARE trying to pay. They used forbearance (which the government told them to use), came out of it, and were told they couldn't just resume payments — they had to pay everything back immediately or accept a modification at 3x–4x their original rate. The partial claim lets them resume at their original rate with missed payments deferred. It's not forgiveness — it costs the VA $0 upfront (repaid when the home sells or refinances). Homeowners with FHA/conventional loans got permanent partial claims and HAF assistance. Veterans couldn't even access HAF because VA disability compensation was counted as income. The people who served got the worst deal of anyone. [If the conversation involves fraud/fiscal responsibility talking points, add: "Businesses got PPP loans — many forgiven, many fraudulently. Veterans are asking to resume paying their own mortgages at the rate they earned. That's the comparison."]
"VASP was a bad program. Even Republicans said so."
Yes, VASP had implementation problems — long processing times, servicer confusion, cases falling through cracks. Nobody is saying it was perfect. But "the program had problems" and "we should let 160,000 families lose their homes while we build a better one" are not the same argument. You can fix bureaucratic inefficiency AND keep families housed. Congress agreed — that's why H.R. 1815 passed unanimously. The question is why the VA hasn't implemented it.
"This started under Biden so it's Biden's fault."
The structural gap (no permanent partial claim authority) predates both administrations. Biden's VA created VASP late — should've happened years earlier. Trump's VA ended it without a replacement. Congress fixed the law bipartisanly. The current VA won't implement the fix. Blaming one party lets the other off the hook. Both are accountable. The VA is the bottleneck right now.
"VA mortgages are being gutted / the whole program is gone."
Not accurate. The VA Home Loan Guaranty program still exists and is still issuing new loans. What's gone is the safety net for veterans who fall behind. The loss mitigation waterfall — the set of options to help veterans catch up on missed payments and avoid foreclosure — has a massive gap in it because the partial claim program isn't operational. New loans are fine. Veterans already in trouble have nowhere to go.
"VASP was just one of many options. It was the last resort temporary one."
That's the point. The VA's loss mitigation "waterfall" — the sequence of options for veterans who fall behind — has a massive gap in it. The remaining options (repayment plans, loan modifications) all require veterans to accept today's interest rates, often 3x–4x their original. That's not affordable. The partial claim — what VASP provided and H.R. 1815 makes permanent — is the tool that lets veterans resume at their original rate. FHA has this. Fannie Mae has this. Freddie Mac has this. The VA is the only federal mortgage system that doesn't. When people say "VASP was just the last resort" — yes, and it was the only one that actually worked for families who couldn't absorb a tripled payment. Removing the only affordable option and calling it "just one of many" doesn't make the remaining options affordable.
"This is just rage bait / political propaganda."
Chris Arnold and Quil Lawrence have been investigating the VA mortgage crisis for years — they're two of the most experienced reporters covering veterans and housing in the country. This isn't a hot take. NCLC testified before Congress. The Urban Institute published analysis. The ABA Banking Journal reported on it. The Mortgage Bankers Association's own chair-elect testified. These are nonpartisan and industry sources. The 10,000+ number comes from ICE Mortgage Technology. The 90,000 delinquent figure is from congressional testimony. This is documented.

Section 10

If You're a Veteran Family in This Situation

You are not alone. Here's what to do right now:

Connect + Get Help
  • Join the support group: VASP Program Family Support and Information on Facebook — co-managed by affected families sharing resources, support, and information
  • Tell your story to Chris Arnold and Quil Lawrence: NPR's veteran mortgage contact page — they've been investigating the VA mortgage crisis for years and are actively reporting
  • Call SSVF (Supportive Services for Veteran Families): 1-877-4AID-VET — they help with relocation, moving expenses, housing resources. You do NOT have to sign away legal rights to get help
  • Do not sign Cash for Keys without understanding what you're waiving — it typically requires you to permanently waive all legal claims
Eviction Help
  • National: Call 211 — connects to local legal aid, housing counseling, and emergency assistance in every state
  • HUD Housing Counseling: 1-800-569-4287 — free foreclosure prevention counseling, required for some loss mitigation options
  • Veterans Consortium Pro Bono: vetsprobono.org — free legal help for veterans, including mortgage/foreclosure cases
  • WA State CLEAR Hotline: 1-888-201-1014 — statewide legal aid intake line. Call when/if an unlawful detainer (UD) summons is filed
Document Everything — Here's What and Why
  • What to save: Every letter, email, voicemail, phone call record, text message, and VA portal screenshot. Save servicer correspondence, modification offers, denial letters, and any document with a date on it. Note who you spoke with, when, and what they said
  • Where to file reports (this builds the legal paper trail — it may not fix your situation immediately, but it creates the documented record needed for any future legal action, class action, or congressional investigation):
  • — CFPB complaint: consumerfinance.gov/complaint — file against your servicer for any RESPA violation, failure to respond, or loss mitigation failure
  • — VA OIG hotline: va.gov/oig/hotline — report VA failures, unauthorized foreclosures, or servicer misconduct on VA-guaranteed loans
  • — State Attorney General: search "[your state] attorney general consumer complaint" — file for state consumer protection violations
  • — Your Congressional representatives: Call (202) 224-3121 and file a constituent casework request. This gets logged and creates political pressure
  • Why this matters even if it doesn't stop your foreclosure: Every complaint filed is evidence. When attorneys bring class action or injunctive relief, they need documented patterns across families. Your filing makes the case stronger for everyone.
